Appointment Confirmation Workflow Important
▼
Problem IdentifiedPatients were missing phone calls because they were at work, and staff were cancelling appointments unnecessarily as a result. This leads to lost patient visits and frustrated patients.
Expectations
  • Use text confirmations as the primary confirmation method whenever possible
  • Phone calls should mainly be used for cancellations or rescheduling — not initial confirmations
  • Never cancel an appointment solely because the patient did not answer a phone call
  • Attempt multiple methods before considering a no-confirm cancellation
  • Document all confirmation attempts in the system
Training Points
  • Many patients work during business hours and cannot take calls
  • Text messages are seen faster and are less intrusive than calls
  • A confirmed appointment that doesn't cancel is always preferable to a preventive cancellation
  • If a patient truly cannot be reached, escalate to supervisor before cancelling
🪑
Waiting Room Communication Important
▼
Problem IdentifiedPatients reported waiting long periods without any updates when providers were running behind. This created frustration and complaints, and patients felt ignored.
🚫Never allow patients to sit in the waiting room without updates for more than 20–30 minutes.
Expectations
  • Proactively update every waiting patient every 20–30 minutes
  • Do not wait for patients to ask — approach them first
  • If the delay is unexpected or long, offer water or other comfort
  • If a patient chooses to leave, document it and notify the provider
Approved Communication Phrases
Running Behind"The provider is running a little behind today. We appreciate your patience and will get you in as soon as possible."
Time Estimate Available"We expect it will be approximately ___ more minutes. Thank you for waiting with us."
General Check-In"I just wanted to check in — we haven't forgotten you. We'll have you in shortly."
Training Points
  • Communication dramatically reduces patient frustration even when waits are long
  • Patients who feel acknowledged are far less likely to complain or leave
  • Silence sends the message that no one cares — always communicate

Motor Vehicle Accident (MVA) Intake Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedMVA visits were missing critical billing and legal documentation at intake, causing delays in payment and gaps in records for legal proceedings.
Required Information — Collect Every Item for Every MVA Visit
Insurance & Claim
  • Claim number (auto insurance claim)
  • Insurance carrier name
  • Policy number
  • Effective date of coverage
Legal & Adjusters
  • Adjuster name and contact information
  • Attorney name and firm (if applicable)
  • Attorney phone and fax numbers
  • Date of accident
⚠️If any of this information is missing, do not skip it. Follow up with the patient before they leave or call back the same day. Incomplete MVA intake causes billing failures.
Training Points
  • MVA cases are billed differently from routine insurance — missing info can result in zero reimbursement
  • Legal cases may require documentation months later — collect everything now
  • Always separate MVA visits from routine neurology visits during scheduling when possible

Procedure Scheduling Rules Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedProcedures were being scheduled at incorrect times of day and incorrect days of the week, resulting in specimen shipping failures and invalid EMG results.
Skin Punch Biopsy — Strict Rules
🚫Biopsies scheduled late in the day or on Fridays cannot be shipped in time. The specimen becomes unusable.
  • Schedule Monday through Thursday only — never on Fridays
  • Schedule as early in the morning as possible
  • Confirm the specimen shipping window with the lab before finalizing
  • Same-day shipping is required — the specimen cannot wait overnight
  • If an early slot is unavailable, reschedule to the following Monday rather than a late slot
EMG After Injury — Timing Rule
⏱️EMGs scheduled too soon after injury will not show accurate nerve conduction findings. Results will be invalid.
  • EMGs should not be scheduled earlier than 11–14 days after injury
  • Always verify the exact date of injury before booking
  • If injury date is unknown, hold scheduling until confirmed
  • Communicate this rule to patients who push for earlier appointments
Appointment Type Separation
  • Keep MVA visits separate from routine neurology visits
  • Keep EEGs separate from routine office visits when possible
  • Procedures should have their own scheduling blocks

MRI & Imaging Order Tracking Important
▼
Problem IdentifiedImaging centers reported not receiving orders. Patients arrived for MRIs that were never received, and providers did not have results available at follow-up visits.
Expectations — Track Every MRI Order Through 4 Stages
  1. Order sent to imaging center — confirm transmission
  2. Imaging center confirmed receipt — call if not confirmed within 24 hours
  3. Patient appointment scheduled — document date and location
  4. Results received and available in chart — follow up after appointment date
Training Points
  • Never assume an order went through — always confirm
  • If the imaging center does not confirm receipt in 24 hours, call them directly
  • Document every step in the patient's chart as it happens
  • Providers need imaging results before follow-up visits — timing matters

Pre-Visit Chart Preparation Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedProviders were entering exam rooms to find that labs, radiology reports, consult notes, or pending communications were missing from charts. This caused delays, repeat visits, and incomplete clinical decisions.
🚫Providers should never discover missing information during a patient encounter. All chart review must be completed before the provider enters the room.
Complete Pre-Visit Chart Review Checklist
  • Labs — Are the most recent results available and reviewed?
  • Radiology / Imaging — Are MRI, CT, X-ray, and ultrasound reports present?
  • Consult Notes — Are notes from all referred specialists present?
  • Hospital Records — Are any recent ED visits, hospitalizations, or discharge summaries documented?
  • Pending Communications — Are there unresolved messages, portal messages, or callback requests?
  • Referral Status — Are all outstanding referrals tracked and documented?
  • EMG Tables — Are EMG tables printed, scanned, and verified?
  • Medication List — Has the full medication list been verified (name, dose, frequency, formulation)?
  • Allergies — Is the allergy list complete, including EpiPen inquiries?
Training Points
  • Missing information discovered during a visit slows down every subsequent patient
  • Providers who must search for records mid-visit lose trust in the support team
  • Chart prep is one of the highest-value tasks an MA performs — do it thoroughly
💊
Medication Reconciliation Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedMedication lists contained incorrect doses, wrong frequencies, duplicate entries, and missing medications. Parkinson's medications in particular had repeated dosing errors — which can have serious clinical consequences.
Most Common Medication Errors to Watch For
  • Incorrect dose listed (e.g., 25mg entered instead of 250mg)
  • Wrong frequency (e.g., "daily" instead of "three times daily")
  • Duplicate medication entries under different names
  • Medications the patient stopped taking still listed as active
  • New medications started by another provider not added
  • Incorrect formulation (e.g., regular vs. extended release)
  • Incorrect Parkinson's medication doses — extremely high-risk
⚠️
High-Risk Medications — Extra Verification Required:
Sinemet  ·  Requip  ·  Amantadine  ·  Anticoagulants (Warfarin, Eliquis, Xarelto)  ·  Seizure medications (Keppra, Lamictal, Dilantin)  ·  Controlled substances
Medication Verification Workflow — Complete All 6 Steps
  1. Verify medication name — exact spelling, generic vs. brand
  2. Verify dose — confirm with patient and compare to outside records
  3. Verify frequency — daily, twice daily, three times daily, as needed
  4. Verify formulation — regular, extended release, patch, liquid
  5. Compare with outside records — pharmacy printout, previous notes, hospital discharge list
  6. Clarify all discrepancies before provider enters room — do not leave it for the provider to discover
Training Points
  • When in doubt about any medication detail, ask the patient directly and compare with their pharmacy list
  • If a patient is unsure, call the pharmacy before the provider enters
  • Do not assume previously entered data is correct — verify every visit
🚨
Allergy Documentation Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedA patient was carrying an EpiPen for bee sting anaphylaxis, but this severe allergy was not documented in the chart at all. If this patient received a medication or procedure triggering a reaction, the chart would have shown no allergies.
🚫Undocumented severe allergies are a patient safety risk. Every allergy must be identified, verified, and documented at every visit.
EpiPen Rule
  • If a patient carries an EpiPen — always ask WHY
  • Verify the specific allergen and the type of reaction (anaphylaxis, hives, swelling, etc.)
  • Document the allergy with full details: allergen, reaction type, severity
  • Flag as "Severe" or "Life-Threatening" as appropriate
Required Question During Reconciliation"I see you carry an EpiPen — can you tell me what you're allergic to and what happens when you're exposed?"
General Allergy Review — Every Visit
  • Ask the patient to confirm all listed allergies at every visit
  • Ask if any new allergies or reactions have occurred since the last visit
  • Document reactions with specificity (e.g., "anaphylaxis" not just "allergic reaction")
  • Remove allergies only when the patient and provider both confirm it is safe to do so
⚡
EMG Workflow & Quality Control Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedEMG tables were missing from charts, charts were being marked ready for signature before EMG tables were verified, and dictation contained inaccuracies that were not caught before signing.
🚫A chart must NOT be marked ready for signature until the EMG table is confirmed printed, scanned, uploaded, and verified against the dictation.
EMG Table Workflow — All Steps Required Same Day
  1. EMG procedure is performed by provider
  2. EMG table is printed immediately after the procedure
  3. Table is scanned and uploaded to the patient's chart same day
  4. MA reviews the table against the dictation for accuracy
  5. Chart is marked ready for signature only after all items are verified
MA Verification — Check Each Item Against Dictation
  • Number of extremities tested — does the table match what was dictated?
  • Number of nerves tested — verify count matches dictation
  • Severity grading — confirm Mild / Moderate / Severe is correctly documented
  • Dictation accuracy — does the overall summary match the table findings?
👤Shahzaib is the designated EMG documentation trainer and will assist with retraining other MAs on EMG verification standards.
🧠
Neurological Examination Documentation
▼
Problem IdentifiedPreviously documented abnormal neurological findings were disappearing from later visit notes. Normal exam findings were being incorrectly carried forward over known abnormal findings, creating a false record of improvement.
Expectations
  • Import the previous neurological examination into each new visit note
  • Update only the findings that have actually changed
  • Do not overwrite known abnormal findings with "normal" unless the provider has documented improvement
  • The documentation must reflect the actual examination performed that day
⚠️If the prior exam showed abnormal reflexes and today's exam did not test them, do not document them as normal. Leave the prior finding intact or flag it for provider review.
Training Points
  • Carry-forward documentation improves consistency and helps track disease progression
  • Inaccurate "normal" findings can affect medication decisions, disability determinations, and legal cases
  • When uncertain whether a finding changed, ask the provider before documenting

Home Physical Therapy (PT) Setup
▼
Problem IdentifiedStaff were relying entirely on one person (Shahzaib) to arrange all home PT services. When that person was unavailable, the process stalled and patients experienced delays in receiving care.
🚫Home PT setup must not depend on a single staff member. All MAs must know this workflow independently.
Home PT Setup Workflow — Full Process
  1. Identify patient need — confirm provider has ordered home PT
  2. Select appropriate agency — use the approved agency list; verify they accept patient's insurance
  3. Send required documentation — patient demographics, insurance info, provider order, diagnosis
  4. Obtain provider signature on the home PT order if not already signed
  5. Confirm receipt by agency — call or fax to verify they received the referral
  6. Document referral in chart — note the agency, date sent, and confirmation received
  7. Follow up until evaluation is scheduled and confirmed with patient
Training Points
  • Escalate early if an agency is not responding — do not wait days before acting
  • If an agency cannot see the patient within a reasonable time, try an alternative agency
  • Document all follow-up attempts with dates and outcomes

Provider Communication Standards Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedProviders were requesting information before entering exam rooms and receiving no response. They had to ask multiple times or enter without the information needed to care for the patient.
🚫Silence is never acceptable when a provider makes a request. Respond immediately — even if you do not yet have the answer.
Acceptable Immediate Responses When Still Researching
Still Looking"I'm looking into that right now — I'll have an answer for you in a moment."
Waiting on Records"I'm waiting for the records to come through — I'll bring them to you as soon as they arrive."
On the Phone"I'm currently on the phone with the facility to get that information."
Training Points
  • Acknowledging a request is part of patient safety — it keeps the provider informed
  • A simple "I'm on it" buys time and prevents repeat requests
  • If you cannot resolve a request, escalate to a supervisor — do not leave it unaddressed

EMG Scheduling — Injury Timing Rule Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedEMGs were being scheduled within days of an injury. Studies performed too early after nerve injury will not show abnormal findings, resulting in wasted visits, unreliable results, and possible incorrect clinical decisions.
⏱️EMGs must not be scheduled earlier than 11–14 days after injury. This is a clinical requirement, not a preference.
Expectations
  • Always ask the patient for their exact date of injury before scheduling
  • Calculate the number of days between injury and proposed appointment
  • If the appointment would fall before day 11, reschedule to day 14 or later
  • If the injury date is unknown, hold the EMG slot until it is confirmed
  • Educate patients who request earlier appointments — explain the clinical reason
Patient Communication — When They Push for Earlier"The provider recommends waiting at least 11–14 days after your injury for the EMG to give accurate results. Scheduling earlier may mean the test needs to be repeated. We want to make sure we get useful information for your care."
🔬
Skin Punch Biopsy — Day & Time Rules Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedBiopsies were being scheduled on Fridays or late in the day. Specimens require same-day shipping and cannot sit overnight. Late scheduling directly caused specimen loss and wasted procedures.
🚫Never schedule a skin punch biopsy on a Friday or in the afternoon. A missed shipping window makes the specimen unusable.
  • Schedule Monday through Thursday only
  • Schedule in the morning — confirm the exact time window with clinical staff
  • Verify the shipping cut-off time for the specimen lab before confirming the slot
  • If no morning slot is available Monday–Thursday, schedule the following week rather than using a late-day or Friday slot
  • Inform the patient of the scheduling restriction when booking
🚗
MVA Visit Scheduling Important
▼
Problem IdentifiedMVA and routine neurology visits were being scheduled together without separation. This created documentation confusion and billing issues because MVA billing flows through auto insurance, not health insurance.
Scheduling Rules for MVA Visits
  • Keep MVA visits separate from routine neurology visits whenever scheduling allows
  • Keep EEGs and procedures separate from routine office visits
  • Use the correct visit type when entering the appointment
Required Information to Collect at Scheduling
  • Date of accident
  • Auto insurance claim number
  • Insurance carrier name
  • Adjuster name and contact
  • Attorney name and fax (if represented)
⚠️If any MVA intake information is missing at scheduling, flag the appointment for Front Desk to complete at check-in. Do not allow the visit to proceed without this information.

Closed-Loop Referral System Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedReferrals were being sent but not tracked. Urgent referrals were lost or delayed without provider awareness. Patients arrived at follow-up visits with no specialist notes because no one had confirmed they were ever seen.
🚫A referral is not complete when it is sent. It is only complete when all 5 steps below are done and documented.
The 5-Step Referral Completion Standard
  1. Referral sent — fax or portal submission confirmed, fax confirmation printed
  2. Appointment scheduled — confirm with patient that they received their appointment date
  3. Patient seen — confirm via follow-up call or patient report that the visit occurred
  4. Notes received — specialist note received via fax, portal, or mail
  5. Notes scanned correctly — filed under Consultation with correct DOS in patient chart
Training Points
  • Track every open referral in a log or tracking system — do not rely on memory
  • Review open referrals weekly and update statuses
  • If a referral is stuck at any step, follow up proactively
🚨
Urgent Referral Escalation Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedUrgent referrals were being placed into the same queue as routine referrals. Delays went unnoticed and providers were not informed, potentially delaying time-sensitive care.
🚫An urgent referral that cannot be scheduled within an appropriate timeframe must be escalated to the provider immediately — do not let it sit.
  • Urgent referrals require same-day or next-day follow-up — not routine weekly review
  • If the specialist cannot see the patient within the appropriate window, notify the provider immediately
  • Consider alternative specialists or facilities if the first choice has a long wait
  • Document all escalation actions with timestamps
  • Never silently accept a delayed appointment for an urgent referral without provider approval
📠
Specialist Notes & PCP Communication
▼
Problem IdentifiedSpecialist consult notes were missing from charts and PCPs were not consistently receiving copies of specialist notes, breaking the continuity of care loop.
Expectations
  • Obtain specialist consult notes promptly after each appointment
  • Ensure the PCP receives a copy of every specialist note
  • Verify PCP fax numbers regularly — they change frequently
  • If fax confirmation is not received within 2 business days, resend
  • Actively track all missing consult notes — do not wait for the provider to ask

Neurological Clearance Language Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedStaff were repeatedly writing "patient is medically cleared" on forms and letters. This practice implies the neurology practice is clearing the patient for all aspects of surgery or procedures — including cardiac, pulmonary, anesthesia, and surgical risk — which is outside the scope of neurology.
❌Never write: "Patient is medically cleared" or any variation implying general medical clearance.
Correct Template Language — Always Use This Exact Wording
Standard Neurological Clearance Statement"From a neurological standpoint, there is no neurological contraindication to the planned procedure at this time."
When Risk Exists or More Information Is Needed"Neurological recommendations cannot be finalized until the requested records/details are reviewed."
Staff Workflow for All Clearance Forms
  1. Identify what procedure is being requested
  2. Confirm who is requesting the form (surgeon's office? anesthesia? PCP?)
  3. Confirm whether they are asking for general medical clearance or neurology-specific input
  4. If they want general medical clearance — redirect them to the appropriate provider (PCP, cardiology, etc.)
  5. If neurology input is appropriate — use the approved template language only
  6. Send to the provider for review and approval before faxing or releasing
  7. Save completed and signed form in chart under correct category
ℹ️Neurology can only comment on neurological contraindications. General medical clearance must come from PCP, cardiology, pulmonology, anesthesia, or the surgical team as appropriate.
🗂
Correct Chart Categorization & Document Labeling
▼
Problem IdentifiedDocuments were indexed under wrong chart tabs, new duplicate categories were being created, and inconsistent naming prevented clinical staff from finding documents quickly.
Approved Standard Categories — Use These Only
  • Consultations
  • Hospital Records
  • Labs
  • Imaging
  • Procedures
  • Administrative
  • Patient Education
Document Placement Guide
  • Hospital discharge summaries → Hospital Records
  • Emergency department notes → Hospital Records
  • Specialist consult notes → Consultations
  • Lab results → Labs
  • MRI / CT / X-ray reports → Imaging
  • EMG tables → Procedures
  • Patient handouts and summaries → Patient Education
  • Consent forms, insurance documents → Administrative
⚠️Do not create new categories. Do not create duplicate tabs. If you are unsure where a document belongs, ask before scanning.
📅
Correct Date of Service (DOS) Critical
▼
Problem IdentifiedStaff were using today's date (the scan date) instead of the original Date of Service when indexing documents. This caused records to appear out of chronological order, making it difficult to review patient history accurately.
🚫Always use the original Date of Service from the document — never the date you scanned it.
  • Look at the document itself for the date of the service, visit, or result
  • For lab results — use the date the lab was drawn or resulted
  • For imaging — use the date the study was performed
  • For consult notes — use the date of the consult visit
  • For hospital records — use the date of discharge or the date of the specific note
  • If the DOS is unclear, check with your supervisor before indexing
👨‍⚕️
Physician Name Accuracy
▼
Problem IdentifiedPhysician names were repeatedly misspelled in documentation, including on referral letters and forms sent externally. Misspelled names create professionalism issues and can cause confusion with document matching.
  • Verify physician names before entering them in documentation
  • Use the facility directory, NPI lookup, or web search to confirm spelling
  • Do not guess — confirm before submitting any external document
  • Example of correct spelling: Dr. Okoye
  • When in doubt, call the physician's office to verify name spelling and fax number

🔬 Labs, Imaging & Interfaces

Quest interface verification, lab coding accuracy, MRI order tracking, and preventing interface failures.

🔌
Quest Interface Verification Important
▼
Problem IdentifiedLab orders were not transmitting through the Quest interface and results were not returning. Staff were not verifying transmission, so failures were only discovered later when results were needed for a visit.
Expectations
  • After placing every lab order, verify that it transmitted successfully through the interface
  • Check for transmission errors in the EMR before the patient leaves
  • If results are not returned by the expected date, actively follow up with Quest
  • Reconcile all missing results before the patient's next visit
  • Escalate interface failures immediately — do not wait until results are critically needed
Training Points
  • Interface failures are not rare — they must be checked every time an order is placed
  • If the interface has been unreliable, consider calling Quest to confirm receipt of each order
  • Document all interface failure incidents and escalate to the supervisor so IT can be notified
💳
Lab Coding Accuracy Important
▼
Problem IdentifiedIncorrect diagnosis codes were linked to lab orders, causing patient billing problems. Labs were billed without medical necessity documentation, leading to denials and patient responsibility balances that should never have been created.
🚫Incorrect diagnosis codes can create unexpected patient bills and insurance denials. Every lab order must be coded accurately and linked to the correct diagnosis.
  • Use the correct diagnosis code that reflects the clinical indication for the test
  • Verify medical necessity — the diagnosis must justify the lab being ordered
  • Do not use a generic or default diagnosis code without provider confirmation
  • If you are unsure which diagnosis to link, ask the provider before submitting the order
  • Review any lab denials with the billing team to identify patterns
🧲
MRI / Imaging Order Tracking — Full Workflow
▼
Problem IdentifiedMultiple imaging centers reported not receiving MRI orders. Patients arrived for MRI appointments and the imaging center had no order on file. Providers did not have results available at follow-up visits.
Track Every MRI Order Through All 4 Stages
  1. Order Sent — fax or portal transmission; save fax confirmation number in chart note
  2. Confirmed Received — call imaging center if no auto-confirmation within 24 hours; document the name of staff member who confirmed
  3. Appointment Scheduled — record date, time, and location of appointment in chart
  4. Results Returned — after appointment date, follow up to verify report was faxed; confirm report is in chart before next visit
⚠️If an MRI is ordered at a visit and results are not available at the next follow-up, the MA must identify the breakdown point and take corrective action before the provider sees the patient.

Prescription & Credential Security Critical
▼
🚫No prescription should ever be sent under a former employee's credentials. This is a serious legal and compliance violation.
Required Actions When a Staff Member Leaves
  • Disable all EMR access immediately on the last day of employment
  • Remove prescribing access from all systems
  • Remove all saved passwords and autofill credentials from clinic computers
  • Verify the departure is documented in your HR and compliance system
  • Confirm with IT that remote access has also been revoked
No Shared Credentials — Absolute Rules
  • Never share your password with any colleague for any reason
  • Never use another employee's login, even temporarily
  • Never leave your account logged in on a shared computer when you step away
  • Lock your screen whenever you leave a workstation
  • Report suspected credential misuse to your supervisor immediately
Why This Matters
  • Prescriptions sent under wrong credentials create DEA and licensing violations
  • Shared credentials mean actions cannot be accurately attributed — destroying the audit trail
  • HIPAA violations occur when unauthorized individuals access patient records using borrowed logins
✅
Accountability & Task Ownership
▼
Problem IdentifiedTasks were started but not followed through to completion. No one knew who owned specific tasks, and follow-up was inconsistent.
Every Important Task Must Have Three Things
  • An assigned owner — one named person responsible for completion
  • A follow-up process — scheduled check-in or tracking method
  • Completion verification — documented confirmation it was done
Areas Where Ownership Must Be Assigned
  • Referrals — each referral has a named owner
  • Lab follow-ups — tracked until results confirmed in chart
  • Imaging orders — tracked through all 4 stages
  • Prior authorizations — tracked until approved or denied
  • Outside records requests — tracked until received and scanned
  • Unsigned chart follow-up — tracked until signed and sent
📝
Unsigned Chart Workflow
▼
Problem IdentifiedSigned charts were remaining unsent for weeks, and unsigned charts were accumulating without any tracking system to identify what needed follow-up.
Suggested Tracking Solutions
  • Use a shared tracking spreadsheet (e.g., Google Sheets) with filters by status
  • Use checkbox tracking — each chart has a column for "Signed" and "Sent"
  • Set up automated reminders or notifications for charts unsigned past a deadline
  • Assign ownership — one staff member responsible for following up on unsigned charts daily
  • Review unsigned chart count at morning meetings
💬
Communication Group Consolidation
▼
Problem IdentifiedToo many overlapping communication groups were created across platforms, creating confusion about where to send messages and causing important messages to be missed.
  • Consolidate overlapping groups into clearly defined single-purpose groups
  • Assign a named manager for each active group
  • Remove members who do not need to be in a group
  • Define the purpose of each group so staff know where to post what
  • Archive or delete unused groups to reduce clutter
⚖️
Capacity & Legal Questions
▼
Problem IdentifiedFamilies were calling to ask general questions about "mental capacity" without specifying the context, and staff were routing these to providers without enough information to be useful.
Before Routing to Provider — Clarify the Specific Context
  • What type of capacity is being asked about?
  • Medical decision-making capacity
  • Financial decision-making capacity
  • Nursing home placement decisions
  • Legal matters (Power of Attorney, guardianship)
  • Informed consent for a specific procedure
Intake Question for Capacity Calls"Can you help me understand what specific decision or situation you're asking about? Capacity evaluations are specific to the type of decision being made, and the provider will need that context."
ℹ️Capacity is task-specific. A patient may have capacity to make some decisions but not others. The provider needs to know exactly what context they are evaluating before the appointment.

Quality Philosophy
▼
✅This process is not about blame. Every error documented in this manual exists to help us build a better system — not to punish individuals.
The Purpose of Quality Reviews Is To:
  • Improve systems and standardize workflows
  • Improve staff training and onboarding
  • Reduce repeated mistakes across the team
  • Identify process gaps before they harm patients
  • Improve patient care and patient experience
  • Build better teamwork and communication
  • Create accountability without creating fear
  • Strengthen the clinic's overall reliability
💡When a mistake happens, the first question should be: "What in our system allowed this to happen?" — not "who made this mistake?"

Medication Refill & Denial Workflows
▼
Problem IdentifiedPatients reported refill requests going unresolved for extended periods. Denied medications were left with no follow-up, leaving patients without needed medications.
Refill Request Workflow
  • Address every refill request within 1 business day of receipt
  • If the refill requires provider review, route immediately — do not queue
  • If the refill is approved, confirm it was sent to the pharmacy
  • Escalate any request unresolved beyond 24 hours to the supervisor
  • Document all refill request communications with date and outcome
Denied Medication Workflow — All 5 Steps Required
  1. Review the denial — understand the reason (formulary, prior auth, quantity limit)
  2. Check for prior authorization — initiate PA immediately if applicable
  3. Contact provider — present denial and ask for alternative options
  4. Notify patient — communicate the denial and timeline for resolution
  5. Document all actions — every step must be recorded with date and outcome
